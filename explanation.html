Explanation

Observation Information

Initially, an interview-based study was considered due to the effectiveness of completing the study virtually, though one worry was that the data might be biased, which creates inaccurate data. Blommaert (2010) explains that ethnography yields data that emphasises tradition, which may mean that data produced will be different from other data types (Blommaert, 2010). This may mean that ethnography would be a better research method for the investigation as it will produce broader results, giving a better understanding of the participants involved.

Blommaert (2010) breaks up ethnographic research into three sequences. Sequences one, two and three being prior to research, during research and after the research, respectively (Blommarert, 2010). During sequence one, the researcher should create a set of questions to understand possible items to research. Sequence two involves perceiving what is happening and embracing rapidly changing patterns in data. Sequence three summarises data and supports any arguments made with the data collected. Blommaert explains it as "telling a story" (Blommaert, 2010).

The unstructured nature of the ethnographic study is good for our research into the usage of Ubuntu. Though, our research does have a few physical restraints. Currently, due to the worldwide Covid-19 pandemic, this research must be conducted online, limiting the number of participants involved. This may mean that the research cannot follow ethnography strictly and may have to include a mix of other methods such as interviewing.

Research Information

While undertaking research, various guidelines were followed to ensure all participants involved in the research were treated equally. All participants were given a participant information sheet before commencing the research activities. A consent form was also required to be signed to ensure that all participants understood the observation process.

All participants were informed that their participation was completely voluntary and that withdrawal from the research was permitted at any time during the process. Once the findings have been finalised, however, removal of participant data may not be possible. Complete discretion will be used when analysing and providing participant data in the research report. Throughout the observation, the focus will primarily be on the technology itself and how it interacts with participants of various backgrounds, rather than focusing on participants and their individual set of skills. This promotes healthy observation where participants do not feel embarrassed for not completing a specific task; instead, this will be associated with poor design implementation on behalf of the operating system. There are no foreseen psychological or physical risks associated with this research.

The data analysis method utilised for our research is a type of conversation analysis, where researchers are interested in capturing details on conversations naturally unfolding (Hesse-Biber & Leavy, 2006). In the case of this research, participants will be asked to speak aloud on their thoughts while using the Ubuntu operating system, which will then be further analysed post-interview to gather in-depth information regarding their experience with Ubuntu.

Method of Observation

Initially, we planned our research based on interviewing our participants. We decided to follow the qualitative ethnographic study method, with a few modifications, given how broad our research is. The chosen method of study included participant observation combined with interview questions to get a further understanding of what the user was performing during a target 45-minute session.

All participants are introduced with some initial questions: (a) "How long have you been using Microsoft Windows for? (b) What do you use Microsoft Windows for?” and (c) “Have you ever used another operating system?" These questions aim to help us understand the current expertise of each participant. The ideal participant is someone with extensive experience with Microsoft Windows but no experience in another operating system.

The first five minutes of the research introduces participants to the Ubuntu operating system, where they are told to become acquainted with the system. During this time, the research will consist of pure observation. The aim is to give us an initial understanding of the participant's general knowledge of the software.

To meet the time limit of the research, participants are prompted with three general tasks and asked to think aloud while completing the tasks. Each task is given once the previous task is completed. The tasks consist of (a) “Try to change your screen resolution.” (b) “Install your favourite browser”, and (c) “Change your desktop background picture”.

The participants are given no advice or feedback when completing the tasks, as only observation will occur. This observation aims to see how participants navigate the unfamiliar operating system. When ending the research, participants are asked to provide a general statement on their experience and whether they would recommend it to another user, allowing us to see whether the user experience was good. The combination of ethnographic and prompt questions drives the research and allows us to collect significant data from just three participants within 45 minutes.
